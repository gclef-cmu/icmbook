# Preface

This book is the companion text for **15-322 / 15-622 Introduction to Computer
Music** at Carnegie Mellon University. It introduces the ideas, techniques, and
tools used to make music with computers: how sound is represented digitally, how
it is synthesized and transformed, and how musical structure can be described and
generated by code.

Throughout the book we use **Pyquist**, a Python audio library developed for this
course. Pyquist lets us move quickly from a mathematical idea to a sound you can
actually hear, and every concept here is paired with code you can run and modify.

## Who is this book for?

This book is written for students who are curious about sound and music and who
have some programming experience, but who do not necessarily have a background in
audio engineering, digital signal processing, or music theory. Where a topic
requires mathematical or musical background, we introduce it as needed and
collect supporting material in the {doc}`appendix <appendix/intro>`.

## What you will learn

The book is organized into four parts:

1. **Foundations of Digital Audio** — what sound is, how computers represent it,
   and how to get started making sound with Pyquist.
2. **Sound Synthesis** — generating and shaping sound from scratch: oscillators,
   envelopes, modulation, subtractive synthesis, sampling, and physical models.
3. **Composition and Control** — representing musical structure: MIDI, scores,
   sequencing, and algorithmic composition.
4. **Effects and Spectral Processing** — transforming sound: delays, reverberation,
   and frequency-domain techniques.

A dedicated {doc}`Pyquist reference <pyquist/intro>` documents the library, and
the {doc}`Course Information <course/website>` section links to the syllabus,
schedule, and assignments.

## How to use this book

Each chapter mixes explanation, runnable code, and exercises. Read actively: run
the examples, change parameters, and listen to the results. The exercises at the
end of each chapter are the best way to check your understanding.

```{tip}
Every code example is meant to be run. If something is unclear, the fastest way
to understand it is usually to change a number and listen to what happens.
```
